# Positioning Against Adjacent Frameworks

CAP is positioned in the space of frameworks that take lived, first-person experience seriously as a unit of analysis. It does not compete with physics, neuroscience, or cognitive science at their levels of abstraction. It competes with — and supplements — frameworks that try to describe *how lived experience is organized*.

This document maps CAP against the closest neighbors and identifies what each neighbor leaves unaddressed.

---

## Comparative Table

| Framework | Central Claim | What's Missing |
|---|---|---|
| **Goodman** (constructivism) | Worlds are made by symbolic versions and descriptions | No transition cost, no budget discipline |
| **Varela** / enactivism | Worlds are enacted through embodied coupling between organism and environment | No typed operator grammar, no risk gating |
| **Clark** / predictive processing | Perception and action are linked through prediction and error correction | No operator admissibility, no budget-limited transitions |
| **Friston** / active inference | Behavior and brain function are organized around free-energy minimization | No typed operator grammar for life transitions, no leakage screening |
| **Lieder & Griffiths** / resource rationality | Cognition is bounded by computational resources | Works with resources, but not as phenomenology of lived experience |
| **CAP** | **Lived worlds are navigated through budget-limited, telemetry-gated operators** | — |

---

## What CAP Adds

CAP does not claim that the adjacent frameworks are wrong. It claims they are **incomplete in a specific, operationally important way**: none of them treats the *cost of leaving a state* as a first-class structural property of the state itself.

### Against Goodman

Goodman's constructivism is correct that worlds are organized through symbolic versions. CAP accepts this. But Goodman's worlds are infinitely permeable to redescription — there is no structural cost to switching between symbolic versions. CAP adds: redescriptions are operators, operators have risk weights, and risk weights interact with observer budgets. The world a depleted observer can construct is not the world a full-budget observer can construct, even if both have access to the same symbolic vocabulary.

### Against Varela / Enactivism

Enactivism is correct that experience is constituted through embodied coupling. CAP accepts this. But enactivism does not type its operators. It describes coupling without distinguishing Inversion from Boundary from Closure as moves with different risk profiles and admissibility conditions. CAP adds the operator grammar that enactivism leaves implicit.

### Against Clark / Predictive Processing

Predictive processing is correct that perception and action are organized around prediction and error. CAP accepts this. But predictive processing has no concept of operator admissibility — it does not gate which actions can fire based on the agent's current resource state. A prediction-error minimizing agent in active inference frameworks does not automatically refuse to act when its budget is exhausted. CAP makes that refusal structural.

### Against Friston / Active Inference

Active inference is correct that behavior can be organized around free-energy minimization. CAP accepts this as a useful formalism. But active inference does not give you a typed grammar for life transitions: it does not tell you that Граница (Boundary) is preferable to Разрыв (Break) when telemetry is Loaded, or that Усиление (Pump) on a Leaking node accelerates damage. Active inference describes a kind of optimization; CAP describes the operator alphabet that the optimization has to choose from.

### Against Lieder & Griffiths / Resource Rationality

Resource rationality is the closest neighbor. It explicitly treats cognition as bounded by computational resources. CAP accepts this orientation completely. The difference is in what "resource" means and where it is measured. Resource rationality is about the *cost of computation*. CAP is about the *cost of lived transitions* — emotional, cognitive, bodily, social, material, and rollback. The framework operates on the felt budget of an embodied observer, not the compute budget of a reasoning system.

---

## Why CAP Is Not Just a Combination

It would be tempting to read CAP as "active inference + resource rationality + a vocabulary." This would be a mistake. CAP introduces three primitives that none of the adjacent frameworks have:

1. **Transition cost as a structural property of state**: a state's cost-to-leave is part of its identity, not a downstream calculation
2. **Operator admissibility as a hard gate**: operators that fail admissibility are blocked, not just discouraged
3. **Telemetry as gate, not signal**: observable signals do not just inform the model — they determine what the model is permitted to output

These are structural commitments, not metaphors borrowed from adjacent frameworks. They produce behavior that the adjacent frameworks do not produce: a CAP analysis can refuse to recommend an operator because the observer cannot afford it, and that refusal is part of the analysis, not an afterthought.

---

## Why CAP Does Not Reject the Adjacent Frameworks

CAP is additive, not replacement. A complete description of any specific lived situation may need:

- Goodman's vocabulary to describe the symbolic version active in the situation
- Varela's vocabulary to describe the embodied coupling
- Clark's or Friston's vocabulary to describe the prediction landscape
- Lieder's vocabulary to describe computational bounds
- **CAP's vocabulary to describe what it costs to move and which operator can fire**

CAP fills a gap. It does not claim the gap was the only thing missing.

---

## Boundary Against the Hard Problem

CAP does not claim to solve the hard problem of consciousness. It does not derive experience from physical processes, and it does not derive physical processes from experience. First-person experience is taken as **primitive** — the starting condition under which the model becomes possible — not as something the model produces or explains.

```text
Experience is not inferred by the model.
Experience is the condition under which the model becomes possible.
```

This is not evasion. It is a deliberate scope choice. A framework that tries to operate on lived experience can either start from experience as given or attempt to derive it. CAP starts from experience as given, on the grounds that the alternative has not produced an operationally useful framework in centuries of trying. This stance is examined further in [`primitive_experience.md`](./primitive_experience.md).

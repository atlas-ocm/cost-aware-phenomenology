# Primitive Experience as Starting Condition

CAP takes first-person experience as **primitive** — not as something the framework derives, but as the condition under which the framework becomes possible. This document explains what that means, why CAP makes that choice, and what it deliberately does not try to do.

---

## The Starting Condition

```text
Experience is not inferred by the model.
Experience is the condition under which the model becomes possible.
```

A framework that operates on lived experience faces a binary choice:

1. **Derive experience** from something more fundamental (physical processes, computation, information patterns) and then describe it from the derivation
2. **Take experience as given** and operate on its structure without trying to derive it

CAP takes option 2. The reason is operational, not metaphysical: option 1 has been pursued for centuries without producing an operator-grade framework that an embodied agent can use to navigate their next 24 hours. Option 2 makes no metaphysical claim that experience cannot be derived — it simply declines to wait for that derivation before doing useful work.

---

## What "Primitive" Means Here

Three properties are taken as given when CAP starts:

### 1. First-person givenness

Experience is given from somewhere — a perspective, a here-and-now, a point of view. The givenness is not constructed by the framework. It is what the framework operates on.

### 2. Pre-reflective self-awareness

The observer does not have to first reflect on themselves before noticing they are observing. Self-presence precedes reflection. This is what allows operators like ReverseTrace to reach into past states without requiring an explicit theory of memory.

### 3. Embodied subjectivity

Experience is always experienced *by* a body, *in* a body, *through* a body. There is no disembodied operator routing in CAP. Even when a node is described as "purely cognitive," the cost of moving it is paid in a body that has fatigue, attention bandwidth, and recovery time.

These three are taken as conditions of the framework. They are not theorems the framework proves.

---

## Why CAP Doesn't Try to Solve the Hard Problem

The hard problem of consciousness asks: why is there subjective experience at all, given that physical processes seem describable without it?

CAP does not answer this question. Several reasons:

**Operational scope mismatch.** The hard problem operates at the metaphysical level. CAP operates at the level of "what should the observer do in the next hour given their current budget." A correct answer to the hard problem would not change a single CAP operator recommendation. The two questions are at incompatible scales.

**No required commitment.** A CAP user can be a physicalist, a panpsychist, a dualist, an idealist, or undecided. The framework's operations work regardless. Forcing a metaphysical commitment would narrow the framework's user base without improving its operations.

**Risk of importing untested ontology.** Frameworks that try to solve the hard problem and use the solution as scaffolding tend to import strong ontological commitments that then leak into operational claims. CAP avoids this by refusing to take a position. The cost is philosophical thinness; the benefit is operational portability.

---

## What This Does Not Mean

Taking experience as primitive does not mean:

- **Experience is mystical or supernatural.** It just means the framework starts from it rather than deriving it.
- **Other approaches are wrong.** Derivation projects may succeed eventually. CAP does not depend on whether they do.
- **Subjective reports are infallible.** Telemetry can contradict the observer's self-report, and when it does, telemetry generally wins. Taking experience as primitive does not mean trusting every claim made about it.
- **The framework is closed to revision.** New operators, new domains, new telemetry signals can be added. The starting condition is what does not change; the operational layer is open.

---

## The Observer's Position in the System

CAP includes the observer (analyst, LLM, user) as a variable in the system. The observer is not neutral. The observer has:

- a telemetry state
- a budget
- a risk profile

These affect the reliability of the analysis. Observer overload, observer bias, and observer budget depletion are explicit states that can be logged and corrected for. A CAP analysis produced by an observer in Breach state is not reliable. This is not a moral judgment — it is a systems property.

This treatment of the observer as a measurable variable is what allows CAP to take experience as primitive without falling into pure subjectivism. The framework respects what the observer reports while still being able to correct for the observer's current state.

---

## Continuity as a Property of the Observer

The framework assumes the observer has continuity — that the person making a decision now is in some operative sense the same person who will receive the consequences. CAP does not need this continuity to be metaphysically deep; it only needs it to be operationally stable across the cycle in which an operator is applied.

When continuity is disrupted (severe trauma, dissociation, intoxication, acute crisis), CAP's normal operation is suspended. Specifically, in Breach telemetry state with critical budget, the framework permits only stabilizers (Pause, Fixation) and explicitly refuses high-impact operators. This is the framework's recognition that some states are not states from which normal route engineering is possible.

---

## Compression

```text
Experience is given.
The observer has continuity, telemetry, and budget.
The framework starts here.
It does not try to derive experience or solve the hard problem.
It tries to make the next operator the observer fires
match what the observer can actually afford.
```

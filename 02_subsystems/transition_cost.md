# Transition Cost

Transition cost is the first-class concept that distinguishes CAP from adjacent frameworks. This document defines it, decomposes it, and explains why it cannot be reduced to existing concepts in enactivism, predictive processing, or active inference.

---

## Definition

> **Transition cost** is the total resource expenditure required for an observer to move a node from its current state to a target state via a specific operator, accounting for the probability of operator failure and the consequences of that failure.

Transition cost is **a structural property of the (state, observer, operator) triple**, not of the state alone. The same node in the same state has different transition costs for different observers and different operators.

Formally:

```text
TransitionCost(node, current_state, target_state, operator, observer) =
    Σ ResourceCost_i(operator, observer)
  + RiskWeight(operator) × FailureCost(node, current_state)
  + RollbackCost(operator)
  + CrossDomainDrain(operator, observer)
```

This is not a metric to be minimized in isolation. It is the quantity that has to fit inside the observer's budget for the operator to be admissible.

---

## Components

### 1. Emotional cost

The emotional capacity required to execute the operator, including:
- the affect generated by performing the operator (e.g., grief from Closure, fear from Break)
- the affect required to remain present during execution
- the post-operator emotional residue that will load the next cycle

### 2. Cognitive cost

The cognitive bandwidth required to execute the operator, including:
- attention required during execution
- working memory load during planning
- decision fatigue from selecting between operators
- ongoing monitoring overhead if the operator is reversible

### 3. Bodily cost

The somatic resources spent on the operator, including:
- direct energy expenditure
- sleep debt accumulated
- physical recovery time
- somatic markers (headache, gut tension, fatigue) that act as telemetry signals

### 4. Social cost

The relational and reputational resources spent or risked, including:
- social goodwill spent
- relational debt incurred
- reputation effects
- third-party reaction loading (other people who will react to the operator)

### 5. Rollback risk

The cost of undoing the operator if it fails. Some operators are irreversible (Break, Closure). For these, rollback cost is effectively the cost of constructing a new path from the post-operator state, which may be much higher than the cost of the operator itself.

### 6. Cross-domain drain

The cost paid in one domain that depletes resources in another. A boundary set in the Work domain may consume relational goodwill in the Family domain. A health intervention may consume time budget needed for the Project domain. CAP requires this leakage to be made explicit because it is the most common source of cycle budget overruns.

---

## Why This Is Novel

### Not in Enactivism

Enactivism describes how worlds are enacted through embodied coupling. It does not type the cost of the coupling. A movement and a ritual and a dissolution are all "enactments"; enactivism does not provide a calculus for asking which one is affordable to this body in this hour.

### Not in Predictive Processing

Predictive processing minimizes prediction error. It treats prediction error itself as the cost function. CAP rejects this as the only cost function: prediction error can be very low while transition cost is very high (a perfectly predictable bad outcome is not therefore cheap to live through). CAP separates *epistemic* cost (prediction error) from *transition* cost (the structural expense of moving).

### Not in Active Inference

Active inference treats free-energy minimization as the unifying optimization. Free energy can be minimized by changing predictions (perception) or by changing the world (action). CAP accepts this formalism but adds: actions are typed operators with risk weights, and the agent has a finite budget that constrains which operators are admissible regardless of free-energy gradient. An operator that minimizes free energy but exceeds budget is structurally inadmissible in CAP.

### Not in Resource Rationality

Resource rationality bounds cognition by computational resources. CAP bounds operators by **embodied** resources: emotional, somatic, social, material, temporal. A computationally cheap operator can be embodiedly expensive (e.g., one short sentence to end a long relationship). CAP measures cost where the cost is actually paid.

---

## How Transition Cost Is Estimated

CAP does not assume the observer can produce exact numerical transition costs. It assumes the observer or a parsing system can produce **risk weights** in coarse bands:

| Range | Zone | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 10–30% | Conservation | Low-cost operators. Suitable in any telemetry state. Stabilizers, holds, small boundaries. |
| 40–60% | Nominal | Standard operating range. Suitable when telemetry is Clean or Loaded. |
| 70–90% | Expansion | High-cost operators. Suitable only in Clean telemetry with confirmed budget. Significant route changes. |

Operators above 90% risk are not recommended within CAP. If a situation appears to require a >90% operator, the correct response is: apply Pause, extend ReverseTrace, and seek additional information to reduce uncertainty before committing.

The numbers are **engineering defaults, not measurements**. They are knobs, not truths. What matters is that the bands are stable across analyses for the same observer in the same cycle, so that a Conservation operator and an Expansion operator are reliably distinguishable.

---

## Connection to Observer Budget

Transition cost is meaningless without an observer budget to compare it against. The pairing is:

```text
TotalRisk(active operators in cycle) <= ObserverUsableBudget × RiskToleranceFactor
```

When this inequality is violated, the observer is over-committing the cycle. The framework's response is automatic and non-negotiable: defer the highest-risk operator to the next cycle, recalculate, repeat until the inequality holds.

For the budget side of this pairing, see [`observer_budget.md`](./observer_budget.md). For how telemetry caps the maximum permitted risk, see [`telemetry_gating.md`](./telemetry_gating.md).

## The Compression

```text
A state's transition cost is what it actually costs the observer
to leave that state via a specific operator.
The cost is paid in emotion, cognition, body, society, time, money,
rollback risk, and cross-domain drain.
The observer either has the budget for it or does not.
The framework respects the answer.
```
